# Section 3 — Key Drivers: Credit Grades, Income & Pricing Risk

## Where Does the Portfolio Actually Lose Money?

---

## Portfolio Overview

- The portfolio contains **10,000+ loans** worth **$163M+**, spread across **7 credit grades (A–G)**.  
- Most exposure is concentrated in **Grades B and C**, which together hold the largest share of total loan value.  
- **Grade C is the largest segment**, contributing ~34% of total loans, followed by **Grade B at ~27%**.  
- Loan size increases with income:
  - Low-income borrowers → ~$10–11K average loan  
  - High-income borrowers → ~$25K average loan  

---

## Where Risk Exists in the Portfolio

### Credit Grade = Strongest Risk Predictor

- **Grade A:**  
  - ~4.76% high-risk exposure  
  - ~65% loans carry zero risk  

- **Grade B:** ~9.16% high-risk exposure  
- **Grade C:** ~12.67% high-risk exposure  
- **Grade D:** ~13.55% high-risk exposure  
- **Grade E:** ~17.81% high-risk exposure (Most toxic major segment)

Grades F and G have very low volume, so conclusions are limited.

---

### Income Is Not a Strong Risk Protector

- Low Income (≤ $40K): ~9.01% high-risk money  
- Mid Income ($40K–70K): ~11.34% (highest risk zone)  
- High Income (≥ $150K): ~9.95%  

This shows **income alone does not prevent default**.  
Credit grade is a stronger predictor than salary.

---

### The Most Dangerous Segment — Low Income + Grade E

- Low Income + Grade E → **22.71% high-risk exposure**  
- Same grade still risky across all income levels:
  - $40K–70K → ~19.68%  
  - $100K–150K → ~17.24%  

Grade E remains toxic regardless of income band.

---

### High Income Grade A Borrowers — Unexpected Risk

High-income borrowers with Grade A show higher risk than expected:

- Low Income Grade A → ~1.96% risk  
- High Income Grade A → ~6.06% risk  

This suggests the system may **over-trust high income**, potentially missing behavioural risk signals.

---

## Pricing & Return Analysis

### Risk-Based Pricing Is Working (At Extremes)

- Grade A → ~6.74% interest (Low risk, low return)  
- Grade D → ~19.11% interest  
- Grade G → ~30.8% interest (High risk, high return)

Return-to-risk ratios:
- Grade A → 0.62  
- Grade G → 2.46  

This shows high-risk loans are being priced correctly.

---

### Middle Grades Are Underpriced

Grades C and D:
- ~12–13% high-risk exposure  
- Return-to-risk ratio < 1 (0.78–0.98)

Meanwhile:
- Grade F → 1.80  
- Grade G → 2.46  

This suggests **mid-risk segments are not priced properly for risk level**.

---

## Strategic Recommendations

### 1. Exit the Most Toxic Segment
Stop new lending to:
- Income ≤ $40K  
- Grades E, F, G  

This segment shows **20%+ high-risk exposure**, making recovery cost too high.

---

### 2. Re-Evaluate High Income Borrower Grading
Do not assign Grade A based on income alone.  
Add checks for:
- Total debt burden  
- Credit utilization behaviour  
- Repayment history  

---

### 3. Reprice Middle Grades (C & D)
Increase interest rates by ~1–2% to better match risk exposure.  
Grades A, F, and G appear correctly priced.

---

### 4. Controlled Growth in Grades B & C
Focus growth in balanced risk-return segments, while filtering out borrowers with:
- Recent delinquencies  
- High utilization behaviour  
- Multiple concurrent loans  

---

### 5. Monitor Risk at Combination Level
Risk clusters when grade and income interact.  
Key high-risk combinations to track:
- ≤ $40K + Grade E → 22.71% risk  
- ≥ $150K + Grade C → 16.27% risk  
- ≥ $150K + Grade D → 14.81% risk  

---

## Key Takeaway

Credit grade is the strongest driver of portfolio risk, not income.  
The portfolio is currently priced well at extreme risk levels, but middle grades represent the biggest opportunity for pricing correction and risk optimization.
